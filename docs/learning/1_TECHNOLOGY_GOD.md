# Learning Goal: Become a Full-Stack Software Inventor

## Who I want to become

The most valuable person in software is not the best coder, not the best business analyst, and not the best product manager. It's the person who understands **both** the business problem **and** what technology makes possible — and uses that to invent products that pure business people would never think of.

This is what the Smartcase team did. They didn't throw out the traditional SDLC — they used it, but the architect drove all phases himself and did it more effectively than a team of specialists each owning one phase. He could do BRD thinking, PRD thinking, SDD thinking, and implementation — and because one person held the full picture, the result was tighter, faster, and more coherent than what a handoff-based process produces.

But what makes someone like that the most valuable person in the room isn't just SDLC competence. It's this: **their knowledge of what technology makes possible is what feeds their business thinking.** They don't identify problems the way a normal business analyst does. They see problems, audiences, and opportunities **that non-technical people would never see** — because they know what new technology can now solve cheaply, quickly, or in ways that didn't exist before. The technology knowledge isn't something they apply in the SDD phase after the business people hand them a BRD. It's what generates the product vision in the first place.

The Smartcase architect walks into a bank's back office, sees how chaotic and low-automated the work is, and tells the business people: "Look — you need better case management. BPM technology exists now that makes this solvable in a way that was impossible or too expensive 5 years ago. We can build it. Do you want it?" The business people didn't ask for it — they didn't know it was possible. The technology knowledge creates the business opportunity. That's what makes them an inventor, not just an implementer.

**I want to be able to do that.** Not just for one product, but as a repeatable capability — for my own startup idea, for a client at a bank, or for any company that needs someone who can go from "here's a problem no one else sees clearly" to "here's a working product."

## What I'm missing

### 1. Technology landscape knowledge (the foundation that everything else builds on)

This is the **single most important gap** — because it limits everything else. Without knowing what technology makes possible, I can't:
- See business opportunities that non-technical people miss (the inventor's superpower).
- Make informed architecture decisions (I can only pick what tutorials recommend).
- Advise clients or teams on what's achievable (I can only implement what they already imagined).

I know Java backend well. I don't know:
- Modern frontend frameworks and when to use which.
- The full spectrum of rendering strategies (SSG, SSR, SPA, hybrid) and what each enables as a business capability.
- Cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, GCP) — what services exist, what they cost, what products they make possible.
- DevOps / CI-CD — how to get code from laptop to production automatically.
- Analytics, monitoring, observability — how to know if the product is working.
- Modern content management, styling approaches, deployment architectures.
- Mobile, real-time, serverless, edge computing, AI/ML as product features — the landscape beyond "Java backend."

**This gap doesn't just limit the SDD phase. It limits the BRD/PRD phases too.** If I don't know that serverless functions exist, I can't envision a product that uses them. If I don't know that SSG can serve millions of readers at zero cost, I can't see the content-business opportunities that creates. Technology knowledge feeds business thinking — they're not separate skills used in separate phases.

### 2. Technology-informed business thinking

The traditional BRD phase skills (identify a problem, define audiences, scope and prioritize) are necessary but not sufficient. A normal business analyst can do those. What makes the inventor different is doing those things **with deep knowledge of what technology now makes possible.**

- Identifying problems that ONLY someone with technology knowledge would see.
- Defining audiences and products that non-technical people would never think of — because they don't know what's now cheap, fast, or possible for the first time.
- Scoping and prioritizing based on what's technically easy vs. hard, not just what the business wants most.
- Telling business people: "you're asking for X, but technology now makes Y possible, which is a much better solution to your real problem."

### 3. End-to-end execution through the SDLC
- Going from a blank page to a live product, alone, through all phases.
- Using the traditional SDLC (BRD → PRD → SDD → TSD → implementation) but driving all phases personally, making the process more effective than when specialists each own one phase.
- Making architecture and technology decisions with confidence, not just guessing.
- Understanding trade-offs well enough to choose honestly and explain why.

## The challenge with enterprise templates (and why they're still worth using)

I started with BRD/PRD/SDD/TSD templates from enterprise (bank-style) SDLC. The inventor type doesn't discard these — he uses the traditional phases but drives them all personally, making the process more effective than when specialists each own one phase. The SDLC is a proven discipline; the inventor just removes the handoff overhead and injects technology knowledge into every phase.

**What the templates do well:**
- They force you to ask the right questions at each phase.
- They create documents you can refer back to and learn from.
- They prevent skipping important thinking ("I'll just start coding" syndrome).
- They're the language that enterprise teams speak — knowing them is valuable for working in banks, corporations, consulting.

**Where they fall short for the inventor's approach:**
- **They assume role separation.** In a bank, the BA writes the BRD, the PM writes the PRD, the architect writes the SDD. The templates are designed for handoffs between roles. The inventor drives all phases, which means the sharp boundaries between phases become more fluid — technology thinking informs BRD thinking, business thinking shapes SDD decisions.
- **They don't teach the "what's possible" knowledge.** The SDD template says "list your technology stack" but doesn't help you understand the landscape of options or what each option enables as a business capability.
- **They don't capture where the vision comes from.** The BRD template asks you to document a vision — but the inventor's vision comes from the intersection of "what people need" and "what technology now makes possible." That intersection isn't a phase in the SDLC. It's a capability that the person brings to the process.

## What I actually need from this learning process

### At each SDLC phase, I need to learn:

**BRD phase (Business thinking) — but powered by technology knowledge**
- The traditional skills: identify a problem, define audiences, define success, scope and prioritize.
- **The inventor's addition:** see problems and opportunities that only someone with technology knowledge would see. "Here's a pain point in industry X, AND here's why modern technology Y makes a solution viable now when it wasn't before." The technology landscape knowledge (gap #1) directly feeds this phase — the deeper the tech knowledge, the better the business thinking.
- *I've practiced the traditional BRD skills on my website project. The "technology-informed" layer will grow as my technology landscape knowledge expands with each project.*

**PRD phase (Product thinking) — shaped by knowing what's buildable**
- The traditional skills: user stories, acceptance criteria, milestones, feature prioritization.
- **The inventor's addition:** knowing what's technically easy vs. hard shapes what you put in v1. A PRD written by someone who knows that SSG makes a blog nearly free to operate will make different scoping decisions than a PRD written by someone who assumes "a website needs a server." Technical knowledge makes the PRD more realistic, more ambitious in the right places, and leaner in others.
- *I've practiced the traditional PRD skills on my website project. The PRD is done.*

**SDD phase (Architect thinking) — this is where I'm stuck**
- The full landscape of architectural options (not just "what my project needs" but "what exists in the world and what each option enables as a business capability").
- How to evaluate trade-offs and make informed decisions.
- How to document decisions so future-me (or a team) understands why.
- **This phase is also where I build the technology landscape knowledge that will make my future BRD/PRD phases stronger.** Learning what SSR enables as a business capability isn't just useful for picking a rendering strategy now — it's knowledge I carry into the next project's business-thinking phase.
- *I've started this with the Technology Decisions workshop document. DP-1 (Rendering Strategy) is the first decision to explore.*

**TSD phase (Tech Lead thinking)**
- How to translate architecture into implementation-ready structure.
- Project layout, coding patterns, configuration, development workflow.
- *Haven't reached this yet.*

**Implementation + Deployment**
- Actually building and shipping the product.
- This is where my Java backend skills apply — but I'll be in unfamiliar territory (frontend, infrastructure, CI/CD).
- *Haven't reached this yet.*

### And across all phases, I need:
- **Technology landscape knowledge.** For each decision point, understand what options exist in the world, what they enable, and when to use them. This is the knowledge that turns a coder into an inventor.
- **Trade-off thinking.** Every choice has costs. Learn to see them clearly and choose honestly.
- **The confidence to decide.** Not "pick what a tutorial says" but "understand the landscape, evaluate against my constraints, and choose with conviction."
